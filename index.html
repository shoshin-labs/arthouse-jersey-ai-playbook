The one-minute map

ArtHouse Jersey already works across public programme, learning, support, venues, and opportunities. AI should sit behind those layers and make each one easier to run — never replace the organisational voice or the final human decision.

1 · Public programme

Help people discover, understand, and book.

Use AI to tidy event descriptions, answer common questions, suggest routes through the season, and keep the website / newsletter / social copy aligned.

2 · Development

Turn research into better asks and better stewardship.

Use AI to prepare donor briefs, prospect lists, meeting notes, funding proposals, thank-yous, reporting drafts, and relationship prompts.

3 · Education and participation

Make learning materials faster to produce and easier to reuse.

Turn exhibitions and projects into workshop packs, captions, accessibility copy, session outlines, and repeatable activity templates.

4 · Operations

Reduce the hidden admin that slows everything down.

Use AI to summarise meetings, answer policy questions, organise internal knowledge, draft procedures, and keep actions moving between teams.

What changes

If you only change four things, change these.

01

From blank pages to first drafts

Let AI get to version one quickly for bids, copy, notes, emails, and internal documents — then staff refine the substance.

02

From memory loss to a working memory

Capture recurring decisions, preferences, and project history so the team does not keep rediscovering the same answers.

03

From ad hoc help to repeatable helpers

Build a small set of named assistants, each with one job: development, programmes, comms, operations, supporter care.

04

From consultant dependence to transfer

Use consultants to design and launch the system, then hand the day-to-day running back to ArtHouse Jersey with confidence.

The helper map

The first agents and assistants to build

Pick a lens to see the cards most relevant to that team. Every helper should have one owner, one obvious job, and one clear boundary.

Development Governance

Development intelligence assistant

Prepares board / donor briefs, tracks prospects, remembers relationship history, and summarises meetings into actions.

Consultants help: shape the data model, connect the CRM, and set the approval flow for sensitive donor information.

Development Comms

Proposal and stewardship drafter

Drafts funding applications, thank-you notes, stewardship updates, and reporting skeletons using the same house tone every time.

Consultants help: build the prompt library, standardise templates, and check that outputs reflect brand and funder expectations.

Programmes Comms

Programme planning copilot

Turns exhibition plans, artist notes, and event inputs into schedules, captions, run sheets, public copy, and cross-promotional lists.

Consultants help: connect the planning workflow to calendars and shared docs so the system stays current without extra admin.

Programmes Operations

Education pack builder

Converts exhibitions and artist commissions into school packs, workshop plans, gallery trails, captions, and accessibility copy.

Consultants help: design the reusable structure and make sure the production workflow is easy for staff to repeat.

Comms Operations

Website and newsletter editor

Keeps event pages, newsletters, press copy, social captions, and public FAQs aligned without creating five different versions of the same message.

Consultants help: define the editorial tone, page structure, and approval steps before the team relies on the workflow.

Comms Governance

Audience concierge

Answers common questions about opening times, venues, access, booking, travel, and how to engage with the charity.

Consultants help: map the knowledge base, test the responses, and keep the bot within safe and useful boundaries.

Operations Governance

Ops memory assistant

Captures meeting notes, decisions, policy snippets, key dates, and recurring tasks so the team can ask simple questions and get a reliable answer.

Consultants help: set access rules, retention rules, and the source-of-truth structure so this does not become chaos.

Operations Governance

Policy and risk reviewer

Checks drafts for missing approval language, safeguarding issues, rights questions, tone drift, or sensitive claims before anything goes out.

Consultants help: establish the risk taxonomy and the review checklist the team can trust.

Where consultants help

Use outside help for the hard parts — then keep the everyday value in-house.

Consultants should not become the organisation’s permanent brain. They are most valuable when they design the system, wire it together, and leave ArtHouse Jersey with a workflow the team can actually run.

Phase 1

Design the operating model

Run a short discovery sprint to identify the highest-value tasks, data sources, approval steps, and staff roles. This is where outside expertise is most useful.

  • Map all recurring work across development, programmes, comms, and operations.
  • Decide what should be automated, assisted, or always manual.
  • Agree tone, rights, privacy, and risk boundaries.

Phase 2

Build the first integrations

Have specialists connect the first helpers to the right systems — website, documents, calendar, CRM, and newsletter workflow — so the team gets immediate value.

  • Set up the shared knowledge base and prompt library.
  • Create the first front-facing assistant and one internal helper.
  • Make sure logging, permissions, and audit trails are in place.

Phase 3

Assure, train, and transfer

Consultants should test the outputs, train staff, document the workflow, and hand over enough structure that the team can maintain it without heroics.

  • Review examples with staff before anything goes live.
  • Train owners, not just users.
  • Leave behind templates, checklists, and ownership maps.

Good consultant work

Architects, integrators, editors, and trainers.

They should accelerate the build, create confidence, and leave a maintainable system behind.

Not the goal

A permanent dependency for every draft or decision.

The everyday value must stay with ArtHouse Jersey staff, especially in development and relationship work.

90-day rollout

Build trust first, then speed.

The first three months should create visible wins and clear habits, not a sprawling technology project.

Weeks 1–2

Find the best starting points

Interview staff, review current workflows, and choose 3–4 jobs that are repetitive, high-volume, and low-risk enough to automate or assist first.

Weeks 3–6

Ship the first two helpers

Launch one internal helper for development / ops and one public-facing helper for FAQs or event support. Keep the scope narrow and measurable.

Weeks 7–10

Connect the knowledge base

Create a small shared source of truth for policies, programme history, FAQs, and standard copy so the helpers can answer from the same material.

Weeks 11–13

Hand over and expand

Document the workflow, train the owners, review the outputs, and decide which next helper deserves to be added — usually education or fundraising next.

Guardrails

The rules that keep AI useful.

1. Human approval for public voice

Anything that changes the organisation’s tone, promise, or public position gets reviewed by a person before it leaves the building.

2. Sensitive data stays controlled

Donor details, staff data, safeguarding matters, and restricted documents should only be exposed to helpers that are explicitly approved for that purpose.

3. Every helper has an owner

Each assistant needs one named staff owner, a clear job, and a stop button. No orphaned automations.

4. Keep a source of truth

When an answer matters, the helper should cite the underlying policy, document, or page so staff can verify it quickly.

5. Start where the pain is obvious

Pick jobs that waste time every week. If the team does not feel the benefit, the system will not survive.

6. Measure adoption, not just ambition

Track time saved, fewer repeated questions, faster first drafts, and staff confidence. That is the real evidence that the tools are working.

Bottom line

Use AI to make the charity faster, calmer, and more consistent — without outsourcing its judgement.

This playbook gives Sam a credible way to start: small helpers, clear ownership, targeted consultant support, and a rollout that builds trust before scale.
